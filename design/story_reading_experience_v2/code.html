The Subterranean Palaces of Gujarat

Journeying into the depths of India's ancient stepwells, where master craftsmanship meets the essential quest for water in arid lands.

By Dr. Aarav Desai

15 Min Read • Oct 24, 2023

Long before modern engineering tamed the flow of rivers, the semi-arid regions of western India relied on a profound architectural innovation: the stepwell, or vav in Gujarati. These were not merely functional reservoirs; they were subterranean temples, community gathering spaces, and marvels of mathematical precision descending several stories into the earth.

Descending into Adalaj Ni Vav is an experience that defies the harshness of the surface world. Outside, the midday sun beats down unyieldingly, but with every step downwards into the octagonal shaft, the temperature drops noticeably. The ambient light turns softer, diffusing through the intricately carved stone pillars that support the galleries above.

"

They are perhaps the most spectacular structures ever built for the simple purpose of collecting water.

— Victoria Lautman, Journalist

info Architectural Anatomy

The Pavilion

Intricately carved entrance structures that served as resting places for weary travelers.

The Shaft

The cylindrical core that reaches down to the water table, flanked by multi-story galleries.

The Well

The deepest point, often associated with spiritual purity and dedicated to specific deities.

The proliferation of these structures reached its zenith between the 11th and 16th centuries. Royal patronage, often by queens, funded these massive undertakings. Rani Ki Vav in Patan, built by Queen Udayamati in memory of her husband King Bhimdev I, stands as arguably the most magnificent example—a monument to love constructed in reverse, descending to the depths rather than soaring to the heavens.

The descent to water

Despite their historical and architectural significance, many stepwells fell into disuse and disrepair during the British Raj, when modern plumbing and pumps were introduced, and the wells were deemed unsanitary. It is only in recent decades that conservation efforts have begun to unearth and restore these submerged palaces, revealing their former glory and reminding us of a time when the architecture of survival was synonymous with profound beauty.
